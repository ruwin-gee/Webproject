E-Waste & Climate Pollution

How discarded electronics are silently driving climate change — and what students can do about it.

What is E-Waste?

Electronic waste, commonly known as e-waste, refers to discarded electrical or electronic devices. It is the world's fastest-growing solid waste stream — and most of it is never properly recycled.

From old smartphones and laptops to broken televisions and household appliances, e-waste encompasses any device powered by electricity that has reached the end of its usable life. As technology advances at an accelerating rate, devices are replaced more frequently, making the problem worse with every passing year.

The Global Scale

In 2022, the world generated approximately 62 million tonnes of e-waste — enough to fill over 1.5 million large trucks placed bumper to bumper around the equator.

Only 22% of this waste is formally recycled. The remaining 78% is either dumped in landfills, incinerated, or shipped to developing nations where informal processing causes severe environmental and health damage.

Link to Climate Change

E-waste is not just a waste management problem — it drives climate change through greenhouse gas emissions, toxic pollutants, and the enormous energy cost of manufacturing replacement devices.

Manufacturing Emissions

Producing a single smartphone generates approximately 70kg of CO₂ equivalent. When devices are discarded early and replaced, those emissions multiply across billions of users worldwide.

Incineration of E-Waste

When e-waste is burned — in incinerators or illegal open fires — it releases harmful greenhouse gases including dioxins, furans, and mercury compounds that contribute to atmospheric warming.

Refrigerants & HFCs

Old fridges and air conditioners contain hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) — greenhouse gases 1,000 to 9,000 times more potent than CO₂. When these appliances are improperly scrapped, these gases escape into the atmosphere.

Figure 3: Informal burning of e-waste releases toxic gases and greenhouse emissions. (Source: vecteezy.com)

Environmental Impacts

Beyond direct climate emissions, e-waste causes widespread environmental damage that compounds the effects of climate change, threatening ecosystems, water supplies, and human health.

Soil and Water Contamination

Heavy metals such as lead, mercury, cadmium, and arsenic leach from improperly disposed devices into soil and groundwater. In countries like Ghana and China, toxic metal concentrations hundreds of times above safe limits have been found near informal e-waste sites.

Biodiversity Loss

Toxic chemicals from e-waste bioaccumulate in the food chain, harming fish, birds, and mammals. Weakened ecosystems lose their capacity to absorb carbon — creating a dangerous feedback loop that accelerates climate change.

Key Environmental Consequences

  • Lead from circuit boards contaminates soil, making land unfit for agriculture
  • Mercury released from screens poisons rivers and marine life
  • CO₂ emissions from manufacturing devices to replace discarded ones
  • Loss of rare earth minerals that cannot be recovered from landfill
  • Air pollution from burning plastics and circuit boards

Student Actions

Students are significant consumers of technology. Small changes in our habits can have a meaningful collective impact on the e-waste crisis.

  1. Extend your device's lifespan Keep your phone for 3–4 years. Use protective cases, update software, and replace batteries rather than buying new devices.
  2. Use certified e-waste recycling points Never put electronics in general waste bins. Universities and local councils provide free drop-off services for old devices.
  3. Buy refurbished devices Choosing a refurbished laptop or phone reduces manufacturing emissions by up to 70%. Platforms like Back Market offer quality-checked devices.
  4. Donate functioning devices Working devices you no longer need can be donated to charities or schools, extending their useful life and reducing demand for new production.
  5. Advocate on campus Push your university to implement clear e-waste recycling policies. Student advocacy has driven real institutional change at many universities.
